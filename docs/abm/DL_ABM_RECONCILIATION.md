# DL vs ABM Reconciliation — Regime-Dependent Predictability

## Purpose

This note reconciles two parallel research tracks:

- **Deep Learning (DL):** empirical detection of predictive signal
- **Agent-Based Modeling (ABM):** behavioral explanation of sentiment structure

The goal is to understand whether both approaches describe the same underlying mechanism.

---

## Summary

Deep learning experiments reveal:

> A **weak, regime-dependent predictive signal** exists in retail FX sentiment data.

Agent-based modeling shows:

> Sentiment is generated by **path-dependent accumulation dynamics**.

These findings are not contradictory, but incomplete when considered separately.

---

## DL Findings (Empirical)

Controlled experiments (fixed horizon, no grid search) show:

- Weak predictive signal (F1 ≈ 0.25–0.50)
- Stable across MLP and LSTM
- Strong dependence on market regime

### Regime hierarchy

| Regime | Signal |
|--------|--------|
| LVTF (low-vol trend) | strongest |
| HVR (high-vol range) | moderate |
| LVR (low-vol range) | unstable |
| HVTF (high-vol trend) | weak |

### Key observation

> **Regime effects dominate pair effects**

---

## ABM Findings (Behavioral)

A minimal agent-based model reproduces sentiment when:

- agents accumulate positions over time
- agents exhibit inertia (resist switching)
- agents reinforce positions when aligned with price

This produces:

- persistent sentiment imbalance
- clustering
- path dependence

### Limitation

The model fails in:

- JPY pairs
- CHF pairs
- macro/flow-driven markets

---

## The Apparent Mismatch

### ABM assumption

trend → accumulation → signal

### DL observation

- signal exists only in **some trending regimes**
- signal disappears in others

This suggests:

> trend alone is not sufficient

---

## Reconciliation

### Updated hypothesis

trend + stability → accumulation → predictive signal
trend + high volatility → breakdown

---

## Interpretation

Predictive signal requires:

1. **Directional structure (trend)**
2. **Temporal persistence (stability / low volatility)**

When both are present:

- sentiment accumulates gradually
- crowd positioning evolves predictably
- weak signal becomes observable

When stability is absent:

- sentiment reacts instead of accumulates
- positioning becomes unstable
- predictive structure disappears

---

## Implications

### 1. Volatility is a gating variable

ABM must incorporate:

- volatility regimes
- persistence dynamics
- regime transitions

---

### 2. Sentiment is conditionally predictive

Not:

> sentiment → returns

But:

> sentiment + regime → weak predictability

---

### 3. DL does not validate ABM directly

Instead:

> DL provides **empirical constraints** on ABM behavior

ABM must reproduce:

- LVTF > HVTF signal gap
- regime-conditioned predictability
- pair-dependent variation

---

## Conceptual Model

The combined system can be viewed as:

market regime → governs stability
 stability + trend → enables accumulation
 accumulation → produces sentiment structure
 sentiment structure → weak predictive signal

---

## What Has Been Learned

- No global predictive signal exists
- Sentiment is not additive to price
- Weak signal exists only under specific conditions
- Stability is as important as direction

---

## Open Questions

- What behavioral mechanism links volatility to accumulation?
- How does persistence vary across regimes?
- Why are some pairs structurally different?
- Can ABM reproduce DL signal magnitudes?

---

## Next Step

Refine ABM to include:

- volatility-dependent behavior
- regime switching
- persistence control

Then test:

> Can ABM reproduce the DL regime map?

---

## Conclusion

Deep learning and ABM describe different layers of the same system:

- DL → detects **where signal exists**
- ABM → explains **why it exists**

Their combination reveals:

> Predictability in retail sentiment is **conditional, regime-dependent, and structurally constrained**.
